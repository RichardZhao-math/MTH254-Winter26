    Subsection Angle between Two Vectors

    Given two vectors, they form an angle between them. But how do we know how big this angle is? The dot product can help us figure this out!
    ğŸ”—
    Letâ€™s say we have two vectors, \(\v{v}\) and \(\v{w}\text{.}\) We call the angle between them \(\theta\) and we can construct the vector \(\v{v} - \v{w}\) as shown below:
    ğŸ”—
    This is essentially a triangle if we drop all the direction arrows. That is, this triangle has sides of lengths \(\|\v{v}\|\text{,}\) \(\|\v{w}\|\text{,}\) and \(\|\v{v} - \v{w}\|\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    A relation between the sides of the triangle and the angle \(\theta\) is given by the Law of Cosines as follows:
    \begin{equation*} \|\v{v} - \v{w}\|^2 = \|\v{v}\|^2 + \|\v{w}\|^2 - 2\|\v{v}\|\|\v{w}\| \cos(\theta) \end{equation*}
    Using the relation with length property of the dot product and FOIL-ing, we obtain
    \begin{align*} \|\v{v} - \v{w}\|^2 \amp= (\v{v} - \v{w}) \cdot (\v{v} - \v{w}) \\ \amp= \v{v} \cdot \v{v} - 2\v{v} \cdot \v{w} + \v{w} \cdot \v{w} \\ \amp= \|\v{v}\|^2 + \|\v{w}\|^2 - 2\v{v} \cdot \v{w} \end{align*}
    ğŸ”—
    We essentially found two ways to represent the quantity \(\|\v{v} - \v{w}\|^2\text{.}\) Equating these two expressions together and canceling stuff, we obtain
    \begin{align*} \|\v{v}\|^2 + \|\v{w}\|^2 - 2\v{v} \cdot \v{w} \amp= \|\v{v}\|^2 + \|\v{w}\|^2 - 2\|\v{v}\|\|\v{w}\| \cos(\theta) \\ \implies \qquad\qquad\qquad\qquad \v{v} \cdot \v{w} \amp= \|\v{v}\|\|\v{w}\| \cos(\theta) \end{align*}
    ğŸ”—
    Observe that we can now solve for the angle \(\theta\) between the two vectors by isolating \(\theta\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} \theta = \cos^{-1} \lp \frac{\v{v} \cdot \v{w}}{\|\v{v}\| \|\v{w}\|} \rp \end{equation*}
    That is, the dot product gives us a way to compute the angle between two vectors!
    ğŸ”—

    Theorem 12.3.9. Dot Product and the Angle.

    Let \(\theta\) be the angle between two non-zero vectors \(\v{v}\) and \(\v{w}\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} \v{v} \cdot \v{w} = \|\v{v}\| \|\v{w}\| \cos(\theta) \qquad \text{ or } \qquad \theta = \cos^{-1} \lp \frac{\v{v} \cdot \v{w}}{\|\v{v}\| \|\v{w}\|} \rp \end{equation*}
    ğŸ”—
    ğŸ”—
    Observe that the angle is being computed using the inverse cosine function. The range of the inverse cosine function is \([0,\pi]\text{.}\) That is, we consider the angle between two vectors to be the smaller angle formed between them.

    Subsection Orthogonal Projection

    Remember that the dot product should tell us something about how much one vector goes in the direction of another vector. You may get the idea that the sign of the dot product tells us whether the vectors point in similar directions (acute angle) or opposite directions (obtuse angle). But we can actually know more! The orthogonal projection of one vector onto another vector, which is obtained using the dot product, gives us a precise way to measure how much one vector goes in the direction of another vector.
    ğŸ”—
    Letâ€™s say we have two vectors \(\v{u}\) and \(\v{v}\) and we cast a shadow of \(\v{u}\) on the light through \(\v{v}\text{.}\) This shadow is called the orthogonal projection of \(\v{u}\) onto \(\v{v}\) and is denoted by \(\proj_{\v{v}} \v{u}\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    But how do we find this projection vector? We can use the dot product to help us out!
    ğŸ”—
    Letâ€™s assume two vectors are pointing in the same(ish) direction, which means the angle between them is acute. The diagram is shown below and our goal is to find the length and the direction of the projection vector.
    ğŸ”—
    Letâ€™s tackle the length first. Using trigonometry (and yes there is a right triangle in there somewhere that allows us to use trig), we see that the length of the projection vector is
    \begin{equation*} \|\proj_{\v{v}} \v{u}\| = \|\v{u}\| \cos(\theta) \end{equation*}
    ğŸ”—
    Next, we need to find the direction of the projection vector. Clearly, the projection vector points in the same direction as \(\v{v}\text{.}\) Yet, we canâ€™t just simply multiply the length by \(\v{v}\text{.}\) If we did that, the length of the projection vector would be off by a factor of \(\|\v{v}\|\text{.}\) To fix this, we use the unit vector in the direction of \(\v{v}\text{,}\) which is \(\v{e_v} = \dfrac{\v{v}}{\|\v{v}\|}\text{.}\)
    ğŸ”—
    Therefore, the projection vector is
    \begin{align*} \proj_{\v{v}} \v{u} \amp= \|\v{u}\| \cos(\theta) \frac{\v{v}}{\|\v{v}\|} \\ \amp= \frac{\v{u} \cdot \v{v}}{\|\v{v}\|} \frac{\v{v}}{\|\v{v}\|} \\ \amp= \frac{\v{u} \cdot \v{v}}{\|\v{v}\|^2} \v{v} \\ \amp= \frac{\v{u} \cdot \v{v}}{\v{v} \cdot \v{v}} \v{v} \end{align*}
    This projection vector is also denoted by \(\v{u}_{\parallel \v{v}}\) since we can think of it as the component of \(\v{u}\) that is parallel to \(\v{v}\text{.}\)

    Definition 12.3.25. Projection of \(\v{u}\) onto \(\v{v}\).

    Assume \(\v{v} \neq 0\text{.}\) The projection of \(\v{u}\) onto \(\v{v}\) is the vector
