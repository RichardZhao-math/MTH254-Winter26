    Section 12.2 Three-Dimensional Spaces: Surfaces, Vectors, and Curves

    We live in a three-dimensional world, yet our study of calculus has so far been largely restricted to the flat, two-dimensional plane. In this section, we will upgrade our coordinate system from \(\R^2\) to \(\R^3\text{.}\) We will see that many familiar concepts, such as the distance formula, vectors, and lines, extend naturally into this new dimension.
    ðŸ”—

    Objectives

    After this section, students will be able to:
    ðŸ”—
    • locate points in a three-dimensional Cartesian coordinate system and identify the octants.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • calculate the distance between two points in \(\R^3\) using the distance formula.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • identify and write equations for basic surfaces in \(\R^3\text{,}\) including planes, spheres, and cylinders.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • perform vector operations (addition, scalar multiplication, and magnitude) in \(\R^3\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • determine vector and parametric equations for lines in \(\R^3\) given a point and a direction, or two points.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • analyze the relationship between two lines in space to determine if they are parallel, intersecting, or skew.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    To discuss stuff in three-dimensional space, we need to first determine how to represent points in \(\R^3\text{.}\) Just like points in \(\R^2\text{,}\) we can use an ordered triple of real numbers \((a,b,c)\) to represent a point in \(\R^3\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    You may guess that we can graph stuff in \(\R^3\) using three orthogonal axes, one for each coordinate. This is indeed the case, and the three axes are usually labeled \(x\text{,}\) \(y\text{,}\) and \(z\text{,}\) following the right-hand rule: if the curled fingers of the right hand are rotated from the positive \(x\)-axis to the positive \(y\)-axis, the thumb points in the direction of the positive \(z\)-axis.
    ðŸ”—
    The point where the three axes intersect is called the origin and is denoted by \((0,0,0)\text{.}\) An ordered triple \((a,b,c)\) represents the point that is \(a\) units along the \(x\)-axis, \(b\) units along the \(y\)-axis, and \(c\) units along the \(z\)-axis.
    ðŸ”—
    To visualize this point, it is helpful to construct a rectangular box with one vertex at the origin and the opposite vertex at the point \((a,b,c)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Recall that the two axes in \(\R^2\) divide the plane into four quadrants. Similarly, the three axes in \(\R^3\) divide the space into eight regions called octants. We call the region where \(x\gt 0\text{,}\) \(y\gt 0\text{,}\) and \(z\gt 0\) the first octant. Unlike how quadrants are usually numbered in \(\R^2\text{,}\) there is no consistent way to number the octants in \(\R^3\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Next, we can talk about the distance between two points in \(\R^3\text{.}\) The distance formula in \(\R^3\) is an extension of the distance formula in \(\R^2\) using the Pythagorean Theorem twice.
    ðŸ”—

    Theorem 12.2.3. Distance Formula in \(\R^3\).

    The distance \(|P - Q|\) between the points \(P = (a_1, b_1, c_1)\) and \(Q = (a_2, b_2, c_2)\) is
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} |P - Q| = \sqrt{(a_2 - a_1)^2 + (b_2 - b_1)^2 + (c_2 - c_1)^2} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—

    A quick proof to show the distance formula in \(\R^3\).

    Let \(P = (a_1, b_1, c_1)\) and \(Q = (a_2, b_2, c_2)\) be two points in \(\R^3\text{.}\) We first construct the point \(R = (a_2, b_2, c_1)\text{.}\) Then, by applying the Pythagorean Theorem in the right triangle \(\triangle PRQ\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} |P - Q|^2 = |P - R|^2 + |R - Q|^2 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    We can see that \(|R - Q| = |c_2 - c_1|\text{.}\) To find the hypotenuse \(|P - Q|\text{,}\) we need to find \(|P - R|\) first.
    ðŸ”—
    To find \(|P - R|\text{,}\) we will need to construct another point \(O = (a_1, b_2, c_1)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    We apply the Pythagorean Theorem again in the triangle \(\triangle POR\) to find \(|P - R|^2\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} |P - R|^2 = |P - O|^2 + |O - R|^2 = (a_2 - a_1)^2 + (b_2 - b_1)^2 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    Hence, we obtain
    \begin{align*} |P - Q|^2 \amp= |P - R|^2 + |R - Q|^2 \\ \amp= (a_2 - a_1)^2 + (b_2 - b_1)^2 + (c_2 - c_1)^2 \end{align*}
    Taking the square root of both sides gives the desired result.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Now that we know how \(\R^3\) works, letâ€™s move on to discussing some other important objects in \(\R^3\text{,}\) including surfaces, vectors, and lines.
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Surfaces

    It is difficult to put a precise mathematical definition of what a surface is. One way to think about it is that a surface is a generalization of a plane in the sense that surfaces can be curved (this is similar to how curves are generalizations of lines).
    ðŸ”—
    The most easiest planes in \(\R^3\) are the \(xy\)-plane, \(xz\)-plane, and \(yz\)-plane.
    • Setting \(z = 0\) defines the \(\boldsymbol{xy}\)-plane.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • Setting \(y = 0\) defines the \(\boldsymbol{xz}\)-plane.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • Setting \(x = 0\) defines the \(\boldsymbol{yz}\)-plane.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Similarly, we can get a plane by setting one of the coordinates to be a constant. These types of planes are called the coordinate planes.
    • The equation \(x = a\) defines a plane parallel to the \(yz\)-plane that intersects the \(x\)-axis at \((a,0,0)\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • The equation \(y = b\) defines a plane parallel to the \(xz\)-plane that intersects the \(y\)-axis at \((0,b,0)\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • The equation \(z = c\) defines a plane parallel to the \(xy\)-plane that intersects the \(z\)-axis at \((0,0,c)\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    There are other types of planes in \(\R^3\) as well. In general, a plane in \(\R^3\) can be determined by a point on the plane and a normal vector to the plane. We will discuss planes in 3D more in detail in Section 12.5.
    ðŸ”—
    Letâ€™s shift our focus to other types of surfaces in \(\R^3\text{.}\) To describe surfaces in \(\R^3\text{,}\) we need some equations to put some constraints on the coordinate(s).
    ðŸ”—
    For example, we can describe the surface of a sphere by thinking about the constraint of the points on it. Imagine there is a sphere in \(\R^3\) centered at the point \(Q = (a,b,c)\) with radius \(R\text{.}\) Then any point \(P = (x,y,z)\) on the sphere must be exactly \(R\) units away from the center \(Q\text{.}\) Using our distance formula in \(\R^3\text{,}\) we have
    \begin{equation*} R = \sqrt{(x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2 + (z - c)^2} \end{equation*}
    We can "simplify" this equation by squaring both sides to get rid of the square root. This gives us the standard equation of a sphere in \(\R^3\)
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 12.2.9. Standard Equation of a Sphere in \(\R^3\).

    An equation of the sphere in \(\R^3\) of radius \(R\) centered at \(Q = (a,b,c)\) is
    \begin{equation*} (x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2 + (z - c)^2 = R^2 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Note 12.2.11. Hey Richard, can we call this a ball instead of a sphere?

    The short answer is no... There is a big difference between a sphere and a ball in mathematics.
    ðŸ”—
    Observing the standard equation of a sphere, we can see that it only describes the surface of the sphere, not the interior.
    ðŸ”—
    Yet, a ball includes both the surface and the interior. An equation of a ball of radius \(R\) centered at \(Q = (a,b,c)\) is given by the inequality
    \begin{equation*} (x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2 + (z - c)^2 \le R^2 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    If you took MTH 252 (or 252Z) with Richard, you may remember he pointed out the difference between a circle and a disk when he taught the Disk Method. This is the same idea here.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 12.2.12.

    Give an equation of the sphere of radius \(3\) centered at \((0,0,-3)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Using the standard equation of a sphere, we have
    \begin{equation*} (x - 0)^2 + (y - 0)^2 + (z - (-3))^2 = 3^2 \end{equation*}
    which simplifies to
    \begin{equation*} x^2 + y^2 + (z + 3)^2 = 9 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Another way to get a different surface is to relax the constraints on the coordinate(s). For example, consider the equation
    \begin{equation*} (x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2 = R^2 \end{equation*}
    We know that this is an equation of a circle in \(\R^2\) (or more specifically, in the \(xy\)-plane) with radius \(R\) centered at \((a,b)\text{.}\) If we consider this equation in \(\R^3\text{,}\) then there is no constraint on the \(z\)-coordinate. That is, \(z\) can take any real value. When we allow \(z\) to vary freely, the circle will "extend" infinitely in both positive and negative \(z\)-directions, forming a surface called a right circular cylinder, a cylinder whose cross-sections are circles and whose axis is perpendicular to the base.
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 12.2.16. Standard Equation of a Cylinder in \(\R^3\).

    An equation of the right circular cylinder in \(\R^3\) of radius \(R\) whose central axis is the vertical line through \((a,b,0)\) is
    \begin{equation*} (x - a)^2 + (y - b)^2 = R^2 \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

However, since we have the constraints \(y,z \ge 0\text{,}\) we only get the portion of the cylinder where \(y\ge 0\) and \(z\ge 0\text{.}\) That is, we get one fourth of the cylinder.

Spheres and cylinders are the two most basic surfaces in \(\R^3\) with a lot more applications (for example, we can construct coordinate systems based on them) so make sure you understand them well!

    Subsection Vectors in 3-Space

    Vectors in \(\R^3\) are super similar to vectors in \(\R^2\) we discussed in SectionÂ 12.1. Two points in \(\R^3\) can determine a vector. Let \(P = (a_1, b_1, c_1)\) and \(Q = (a_2, b_2, c_2)\) be two points in \(\R^3\text{.}\) Then we can define a vector as follows
    \begin{equation*} \overrightarrow{PQ} = \la a_2-a_1, b_2-b_1, c_2-c_1 \ra \end{equation*}
    where the \(x\)-component is \(a_2 - a_1\text{,}\) the \(y\)-component is \(b_2 - b_1\text{,}\) and the \(z\)-component is \(c_2 - c_1\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Example 12.2.19.

    Find the components of the vector \(\overrightarrow{PQ}\text{,}\) where \(P = (-3,-4,2)\) and \(Q = (1,-4,3)\text{.}\) Then sketch the vector.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    By the definition of the vector components we have
    \begin{equation*} \overrightarrow{PQ} = \la 1 - (-3), -4 - (-4), 3 - 2 \ra = \la 4, 0, 1 \ra \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    The sketch of the vector \(\v{v}\) is shown below.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    The vector terminologies remain the same as in \(\R^2\text{:}\)
    • Two non-zero vectors are parallel if one is a scalar multiple of the other.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • Two vectors are equivalent if they have the same components.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • The position vectors are the ones whose tails are at the origin \((0,0,0)\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Example 12.2.21.

    Determine whether or not the two vectors \(\v{u} = \la 1,-2,5 \ra\) and \(\v{v} = \la -2,4,-10 \ra\) are parallel.
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    Since
    \begin{equation*} \v{v} = \la -2,4,-10 \ra = -2 \la 1,-2,5 \ra = -2\v{u} \end{equation*}
    the two vectors are parallel.
    ðŸ”—
    In addition, since the scalar here is negative, we know that the two vectors point in opposite directions.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Just like how we can find the magnitude and the direction of a vector in \(\R^2\text{,}\) we can do the same(ish) thing in \(\R^3\text{.}\) Given a vector \(\v{v} = \la v_1, v_2, v_3 \ra\text{,}\) its magnitude is
    \begin{equation*} \|\v{v}\| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + v_3^2} \end{equation*}
    Also, the vector itself can tell us about its direction. If you really want to work with a unit vector in the same direction as \(\v{v}\text{,}\) you can find it by dividing the vector by its magnitude:
    \begin{equation*} \v{e_v} = \frac{1}{\|\v{v}\|} \v{v} \end{equation*}
    ðŸ”—
    In addition, vector algebra works similarly in \(\R^3\) as it does in \(\R^2\text{.}\) Vector addition and scalar multiplication are defined component-wise. That is, if \(\v{v} = \la v_1,v_2,v_3 \ra\) and \(\v{w} = \la w_1,w_2,w_3 \ra\text{,}\) then
    \begin{equation*} \v{v} + \v{w} = \la v_1 + w_1, v_2 + w_2, v_3 + w_3 \ra \end{equation*}
    and for a scalar \(c\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} c\v{v} = \la cv_1, cv_2, cv_3 \ra \end{equation*}
    Then we can construct new vectors through linear combinations.
    ðŸ”—
    The standard basis vectors in \(\R^3\) are
    \begin{equation*} \v{i} = \la 1,0,0 \ra, \qquad \v{j} = \la 0,1,0 \ra, \qquad \v{k} = \la 0,0,1 \ra \end{equation*}
    and every vector in \(\R^3\) can be expressed as a linear combination of these basis vectors. Using fancy linear algebra terminology, the set \(\{\v{i}, \v{j}, \v{k}\}\) spans \(\R^3\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Example 12.2.22.

    Which vector has the smaller magnitude: \(\v{u} = 3\v{i} - \v{j} - \v{k}\) or \(\v{v} = 2\lp \v{i} + \v{j} + \v{k} \rp\text{?}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    We first find the components of each vector:
    \begin{equation*} \v{u} = 3\v{i} - \v{j} - \v{k} = 3\la 1,0,0 \ra - \la 0,1,0 \ra - \la 0,0,1 \ra = \la 3,-1,-1 \ra \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \v{v} = 2\lp \v{i} + \v{j} + \v{k} \rp = 2\la 1,0,0 \ra + 2\la 0,1,0 \ra + 2\la 0,0,1 \ra = \la 2,2,2 \ra \end{equation*}
    Then we compute their magnitudes:
    \begin{equation*} \|\v{u}\| = \sqrt{3^2 + (-1)^2 + (-1)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 1 + 1} = \sqrt{11} \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \|\v{v}\| = \sqrt{2^2 + 2^2 + 2^2} = \sqrt{4 + 4 + 4} = \sqrt{12} \end{equation*}
    Since \(\sqrt{11} \lt \sqrt{12}\text{,}\) we conclude that \(\v{u}\) has the smaller magnitude.
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—

    Subsection Curves and Lines

    Most curves in \(\R^3\) can be represented parametrically than using a single equation. That is, we use three functions to represent the \(x\)-, \(y\)-, and \(z\)-coordinates of the points on the curve with a parameter \(t\text{.}\) This can be visualized as representing the coordinates of a particle moving through space as a function of time \(t\text{,}\) and the curve is the path traced out by the particle.
    ðŸ”—
    Alternatively, we can construct a function of the position vectors with tips on the curve
    \begin{equation*} \v{r}(t) = \la x(t), y(t), z(t) \ra \end{equation*}
    Essentially, the tips of this "vector function" trace out the curve as \(t\) varies. This is called the vector-valued function. You will learn all the fun stuff about vector-valued functions in Chapter 13. In this section, we will focus on the simplest type of curves in \(\R^3\text{,}\) lines.
    ðŸ”—
    Remember back when you first learned about lines in \(\R^2\text{,}\) you probably remember that we need both the slope and a point on the line to define a line.
    • The slope tells us the direction of the line. Yet knowing just the slope doesnâ€™t tell us a unique line... There can be thousands of parallel lines with the same slope!
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    • A point on the line can pin down the location of the line. Yet knowing just a point on the line doesnâ€™t tell us the direction of the line... We can draw infinitely many lines through a single point!
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    This is also the case in \(\R^3\text{!}\) In order to determine a line in \(\R^3\text{,}\) we need to know both the direction of the line and a point on the line.
    ðŸ”—
    Unlike stuff in \(\R^2\text{,}\) there is no "slope" in \(\R^3\) to represent the direction of a line, since "slope" is a concept that only works in two dimensions. Instead, we use a vector to represent the direction of the line.
    ðŸ”—
    We can find a point in \(\R^3\) just fine, since points in \(\R^3\) are represented by ordered triples.
    ðŸ”—
    Hence, a line in \(\R^3\) is determined by a point \(P_0 = (x_0, y_0, z_0)\) on the line and a direction vector \(\v{v} = \la a, b, c \ra\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—

    Definition 12.2.24. Equation of a Line in \(\R^3\).

    The line \(\c{L}\) through \(P_0 = (x_0, y_0, z_0)\) in the direction of \(\v{v} = \la a,b,c \ra\) is described by
    ðŸ”—
    Vector Parametrization:
    \begin{equation*} \v{r}(t) = \v{r}_0 + t \v{v} = \la x_0,y_0,z_0 \ra + t \la a,b,c \ra \end{equation*}
    where \(\v{r}_0 = \overrightarrow{OP_0}\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Parametric Equations:
    \begin{equation*} x = x_0 + at, \qquad y = y_0 + bt, \qquad z = z_0 + ct \end{equation*}
    The parameter \(t\) takes on values \(-\infty \lt t \lt \infty\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Observe that the parametric equations specify the \(x\)-, \(y\)-, and \(z\)-coordinates of the points on the line as functions of the parameter \(t\text{.}\) These three parametric equations is itself a linear function in the parameter \(t\text{.}\) What we did here is essentially to extend this idea in the notion of vector parametrization.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 12.2.26.

    Find an equation of the line that passes through the point \(P_0 = (1,2,4)\) in the direction of \(\v{v} = \la 5,-3,1 \ra\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    We are given \(\v{r}_0 = \la 1,2,4 \ra\text{.}\) Therefore, an equation of the line is
    \begin{equation*} \v{r}(t) = \v{r}_0 + t \v{v} = \la 1,2,4 \ra + t \la 5,-3,1 \ra \end{equation*}
    for \(-\infty \lt t \lt \infty\text{.}\) The corresponding parametric equations are
    \begin{equation*} x = 1 + 5t, \qquad y = 2 - 3t, \qquad z = 4 + t \end{equation*}
    for \(-\infty \lt t \lt \infty\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Back in algebra 2, you may also have learned that two points can also determine a line in \(\R^2\text{.}\) This is also true in \(\R^3\text{.}\) The trick here is to determine the direction vector using the two points.
    ðŸ”—

    Example 12.2.27.

    Find an equation of the line that passes through the points \(P = (-2,0,-2)\) and \(Q = (4,3,7)\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    Hint.
    To find an equation of a line, we need a point on the line and a direction vector. So there are really things we need to find out.
    ðŸ”—
    The easier part is to find a point on the line (we are given two points on the line!)
    ðŸ”—
    The slightly trickier part is to find a direction vector. Can we find a vector parallel to the line using the two points?
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    A directional vector is \(\overrightarrow{PQ}\text{.}\) Then we obtain
    \begin{equation*} \v{v} = \overrightarrow{PQ} = \la 4-(-2), 3-0, 7-(-2) \ra = \la 6,3,9 \ra \end{equation*}
    We can use either point \(P\) or \(Q\) as the point on the line. Using point \(P\text{,}\) we have \(\v{r}_0 = \la -2,0,-2 \ra\text{.}\) Therefore, an equation of the line is
    \begin{equation*} \v{r}(t) = \v{r}_0 + t \v{v} = \la -2,0,-2 \ra + t \la 6,3,9 \ra \end{equation*}
    for \(-\infty \lt t \lt \infty\text{.}\) The corresponding parametric equations are
    \begin{equation*} x = -2 + 6t, \qquad y = 3t, \qquad z = -2 + 9t \end{equation*}
    for \(-\infty \lt t \lt \infty\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    You may feel like the equation of a line is not unique since we can choose different points on the line to represent the same line (for example, in the previous example, Richard chose the point \(P\) to write the equation of the line instead of point \(Q\text{.}\) He would have obtained a different equation if he had chosen point \(Q\) instead). It is true that the equation of a line is not unique, not only because we can choose different points on the line to construct the equation, we can also pick different vectors that are parallel to each other to represent the direction of the line.
    ðŸ”—
    When things are not unique, it is often helpful to verify that two different equations can represent the same line. Remember that we can determine a line by knowing a point on the line and a direction vector. Then two equations represent the same line if the direction vectors are parallel and they share a point in common.

    Example 12.2.29.

    Determine whether the lines \(\v{r}_1(t) = \la 1, -3, 5 \ra + t \la 1, 2, -1 \ra\) and \(\v{r}_2(s) = \la 0, 0, 7 \ra + s \la -2, 1, 3 \ra\) intersect, and if so, find the point of intersection.
    ðŸ”—
    Hint.
    Letâ€™s just assume that the two lines intersect. If two lines intersect, then they must intersect at a single point.
    ðŸ”—
    Now put on your algebra 2 hat and think about how to find the point of intersection of two lines in \(\R^2\text{.}\) (You would set the two equations equal to each other and solve for \(x\text{!}\))
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
    Solution.
    The lines intersect if there exist parameter values \(t\) and \(s\) such that
    \begin{align*} \la 1, -3, 5 \ra + t \la 1, 2, -1 \ra \amp= \la 0, 0, 7 \ra + s \la -2, 1, 3 \ra \\ \la 1+t, -3+2t, 5-t \ra \amp= \la -2s, s, 7+3s \ra \end{align*}

    \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} 1 + t = -2s \\ -3 + 2t = s \\ 5 - t = 7 + 3s \end{cases} \end{equation*}

    ðŸ”—
    Richard assumes no linear algebra knowledge here, so he will use substitution. Substituting \(s\) from the second equation into the first equation we get
    \begin{equation*} 1 + t = -2(-3 + 2t) \qquad \implies \qquad t = 1 \end{equation*}
    Using \(t = 1\) in the second equation gives \(s = -3 + 2(1) = -1\text{.}\) Donâ€™t forget that there are. three equations in the system but we only used two of them so far! We need to check whether \(t = 1\) and \(s = -1\) satisfy the third equation:
    \begin{align*} 5 - (1) \amp= 7 + 3(-1) \\ 4 \amp= 4 \end{align*}
    We conclude that \(t = 1\text{,}\) \(s = -1\) is the solution to the system, hence the two lines intersect.

    \begin{equation*} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp \big| \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp \big| \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp \big| \amp 0 \end{pmatrix} \end{equation*}
    From here we can read off the solution \(t = 1\text{,}\) \(s = -1\text{.}\)
    ðŸ”—
    ðŸ”—
